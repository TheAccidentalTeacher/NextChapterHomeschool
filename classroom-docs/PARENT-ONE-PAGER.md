Your child's social-studies class will begin a new classroom game this week called **ClassCiv Realms**. Here is what it is, what it is not, and what your child will experience.

## What it is

A 10-session classroom simulation where each student leads one civilization on a shared world map. Across two weeks of class periods, students will make economic, cultural, and military decisions for their civilization — building settlements, negotiating treaties, trading resources, and, yes, occasionally going to war with neighboring civilizations.

The game is designed to teach history, geography, negotiation, writing, and strategic thinking. Your child will write treaty language, casus belli, and reflection prose that will be collected into a printed portfolio at the end of the arc.

## What it is not

- It is not a violent game. Wars are resolved as a score comparison — no imagery of violence, no combat descriptions beyond "conflict" or "battle."
- It is not AI-driven. All civilizations are played by students. AI is used only for generating the post-game epilogue (a one-paragraph narrative summary) for each child's portfolio.
- It is not graded on winning or losing. Your child can pursue three different victory paths: cultural (wisdom), economic (wealth), or military (might). Every child's portfolio is produced regardless of standings.

## What your child will bring home

On Friday May 8, every student will receive a printed PDF portfolio containing:

- The founding story of their civilization, in their own words
- Every treaty they wrote or signed
- A dignifying epilogue — regardless of whether they finished first or last, their civilization is named, honored, and remembered on its own terms

## A note on emotional register

The game has real consequences. A child whose civilization is conquered, betrayed by an ally, or placed under vassalage will feel a moment of loss. That is deliberate — the game's value comes from those stakes being real. At the same time:

- **No child's civilization will be erased from the map.** Our design enforces this at the engine level. The lowest state a civilization can reach is vassalage, where the child still plays each round, still writes their submissions, and still finishes with a portfolio.
- **Losing is expected and recoverable.** There are multiple comeback paths in the game, and every child has a non-violent victory path available from Day 1.

If your child comes home upset about an in-game event, I welcome the conversation. Treat it like you would a loss in any game — acknowledge the feeling, and the civilization they built is still theirs.
